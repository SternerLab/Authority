30039134|This report presents a detailed description and analysis of the reproductive behaviour of the Ten-spined Stickleback, Pygosteus pungitius (L.) In addition to a reproductive ethogram, descriptions of non-reproductive behaviour patterns are also given. The first sign of reproductive behaviour is the appearance of various aggressive activities. As the initial low intensity fighting becomes more intense, it is spatially restricted and territorial aggression develops. In this species, a heterosexual territorial system exists. Both males and females may hold territories, and both sexes exhibit the full agonistic repertoire. By mapping the movements of territorial individuals and the positions of the aggressive encounters between them, it is possible to study the fluctuations of territorial defence during the reproductive cycle. The major fluctuation concerns the increase in size of the male territories during the parental phase. Disputes are settled, not only by actual contact fighting, but also by the use of signal actions. These actions together form a "threat-code". The TINBERGEN-MOYNIHAN threat theory is further developed, on the basis of findings resulting from an analysis of this code. When Pygosteus males are in the possession of a territory, they begin to build nests in the aquatic vegetation. The method of nest-construction is investigated and compared with that of Gasterosteus. Special attention is paid to the nest-material colour-preferences of these two species. Pvgosteus simply prefers lighter-coloured material, but Gasterosteus possesses a more complex colour-selection mechanism. This latter species changes its colour preference for nest material during the actual building of the nest. Once the nest is completed, Pygosteus males are ready to court ripe females. The courtship is a complicated action-reaction sequence, which is fully analysed. It is clearly demonstrated that this sequence is not a simple, rigidly fixed, reaction chain. The courtship may break down at certain stages despite the presence of the appropriate releasing stimuli. Also, it may get out of phase, and short-cuts may be taken. In order to explain these and other findings, an "intersecting gradients" hypothesis is put forward. This suggests that, through the courtship sequence, both the response threshold and the stimulus releasing-power increase. In other words, a stronger sex drive is required to produce a response later in the sequence, on the one hand, and a stronger releasing effect is produced by a stimulus later in the sequence, on the other. Sequence break-down is then explained by supposing that the response-threshold gradient is steeper than the stimulus-power gradient. The point at which these two gradients intersect will be the break-down point. For a courtship to proceed further, sexual arousal must occur. That it does, is demonstrated in several ways. There is a conflict between aggression and sex in the courtship of the male and, during the courting, the earlier stage of which are often repeated again and again, the aggressive tendency is suppressed. This relative form of arousal is of major importance. The derivation of the courtship postures and movements is discussed. The male not only responds sexually to the female, but also threatens her. Threat has been shown to be the result of a conflict between the tendencies to flee and to attack, and it is postulated that a three-point conflict between fleeing, attacking, and mating (FAM) exists in the courtship of the male. Comparative evidence indicates that this is proabbly of widespread occurrence in vertebrates. The exact balance between F, A and M, will, however, vary from species to species. In Pygosteus, the fleeing tendency of males is weak during courtship (fAM-type). The courtship of Pvgosteus culminates with the female laying her eggs inside the nest of the male. She then leaves and the male passes through the nest and fertilises the eggs. Following this consummatory act, there is no sexual behaviour for some time, but there is an immediate resurgence of aggression. This reveals that the sexual suppression of aggression is reversible in this species. It is suggested that pair-formation in other species can be understood as originating from the rendering of this suppression irreversible in respect of one member of the opposite sex. Pygosteus performs no unritualised displacement activities during its courtship, as does Gasterosteus. The presence of eggs in its nest stimulates the male to commence its parental cycle. The female, which has been driven away, takes no part in parental behaviour. The male assists in the development of the eggs by fanning a current of water through the nest-tunnel with its pectoral fins. The frequency and the duration of the fanning activity increase as the eggs develop, and decrease after the young hatch. The male then becomes excessively aggressive. Young are retrieved to the nest by their parent, as in Gasterosteus, but the shoaling of the young, which has been observed in the latter species, does not seem to occur in Pygosteus. The male does, however, construct a nursery for the young in many cases. This consists of a loose mass of plant fragments which are collected together usually just above the old nest, and in which the young can hide. Pygosteus is capable of transferring its eggs from an unsatisfactory nest to a new site, where a second nest is built. The gradual shift, from the association of the male with the old site, to that with the new site, is described in detail. When the young fish are several days old, the parent male shows a decrease in aggression and shortly afterwards parental behaviour disappears altogether and the whole reproductive cycle ends. Another such cycle may then be begun with the formation of a new territory and the building of a new nest. Finally, the reproductive cycle of Pygosteus is discussed as a whole system. The hierarchy theory of TINBERGEN is applicable in general to this system, but not in respect of certain details. These details concern the various nest-actions. The hierarchy theory would postulate a nestbuilding sub-centre controlling nestbuilding actions and a parental sub-centre controlling parental actions, but no such clear-cut distinction seems possible on the present evidence. In conclusion, the relationship between Pygosteus and Gasterosteus is discussed. It is known that the spines of Pygosteus are less efficient as anti-predation weapons than those of Gasterosteus. Correlated with this is the fact that Pygosteus is far more timid than Gasterosteus, restricting itself much more to the densely vegetated areas. In comparing the reproductive behaviour of the two species, it is noted that the major difference between them is the location of their nest-sites. 30039171|An attempt has been made to assemble the information given in this volume on the incubation behaviour of the Herring Gull, and additional information taken from BEER's work on the Black-headed Gull, in a model representing the major principles of the functional organization of the behaviour during a parental shift. A diagram of this model has been given in fig. 61. In this model the occurrences of the different interruptive activities during incubation are explained as the result of interaction of different systems (or parts of such systems) distinguished in the mechanism underlying the behaviour during incubation. As the frequencies of retrieving, re-settling and building were found to correlate with each other as well as with the tendency to incubate, they were considered to belong, with incubation, to the same system (N). The output of this system increases in the course of the breeding season, reaches its maximum during the second or third week of incubation, and decreases thereafter. Incubation dominates in the behaviour of the bird when the output of system N has reached its highest range and when no stimuli disturbing incubation are present. As long as incubation is gong on, other behaviour patterns are inhibited. Incubation can be disturbed by certain stimuli from the environment (like alarm stimuli or the arrival of the mate) and by deficient feed-back stimulation from the nest-bowl. These stimuli induce a tendency to rise in the bird that competes with the tendency to incubate. The first overt reaction to any kind of disturbance is "looking around". This appetitive behaviour may lead to the release of an activity by which the disturbing stimulus is removed, like the retrieval of an egg on the nest rim. It may also lead to the detection of a source of alarm that induces the bird to take off. As long as the bird stays on the nest the conflict between the tendency to leave and the tendency to incubate can bring bout building, re-settling and preening. Building, that seems to correspond to an intermediate range of the output of N, is the first interruptive behaviour to be expected when the tendency to leave rises. It is also likely to occur as the last kind of interruptive activity in a burst after the bird has re-settled. It is thought to be disinhibited by the interruption of the incubation act. Re-settling is an ambivalent expression of both tendencies. Also the collection trip is an ambivalent act, on which disinhibited building is superimposed. Preening, while the bird is on the nest, may occur before or after rising, rarely during settling. It is part of a separate system (P). The performance of preening as an interruptive activity is facilitated by disinhibition when the tendency to leave the nest is strong enough to overcome, at least temporarily, the tendency to incubate. To account for the sensitivity for feed-back information in the incubating bird a comparison unit (CU) is postulated. In this unit the quantitative value of this feed-back (FB) is thought to be weighed against an efference copy (EC), which is a corollary discharge of the input into the coordinating mechanism for incubation. The value of this efference copy, therefore, varies with the strength of the tendency to incubate and consequently the sensitivity of the bird to deficiencies in the nest situation decreases when this tendency drops. However, it is argued that at very high tendencies to incubate the bird becomes again less sensitive to the nest situation because of threshold lowering (fig. 64). Sufficient feed-back from the clutch increases or stabilizes the tendency to incubate, insufficient feed-back stimulation decreases this tendency and thus increases the possibility that the bird abandons the nest. With the application of factor analysis the behaviour patterns can be arranged in two main groups. The members of each group tend to be temporally linked with each other, but are separated from members of the other groups by intervals during which the gull sits quietly on the eggs. One of the groups turns out to comprise re-settling and the manipulation of nest material, the other the activities serving body care. The experiments mainly involved manipulation of the number of eggs in the nest and the placing of eggs on the nest rim in a position from which they could be retrieved by the bird. The tests lasted about 30 minutes during which the bird was carefully watched from a hide, while its behaviour was noted down against a time scale, from the moment it returned in the territory after the disturbance caused by arranging the experimental set up. On a complete clutch re-settling occurred at a rate of about 3 times an hour, independently of the time elapsed since the last resettling. For the occurrence at this rate no typical external stimulus seemed necessary. Building activities could be evoked by disarrangements in the nest structure, preening activities by disarrangements of the plumage. These activities rarely occurred when the bird was sitting on a clutch of three. However, if the clutch was reduced to one egg the frequency of re-settling, building and preening was likely to rise appreciably. Otherwise, it was likely to sit down first and might retrieve or re-arrange eggs afterwards. The interruptive activities shown by a bird, sitting on a clutch deviating from normal, were not caused by an after-effect of the visual situation, but by insufficiency of tactile feed-back stimulation from the clutch. This was proved with glass models, possessing almost no visual and a sufficiently high tactile value.
