10.2307/3668288|A detailed morphological-anatomical account of the ovary structure and placentation in the tribe Pauridiantheae is given using a bicarpellate taxon, Pauridiantha paucinervis subsp. lyallii, and a tetracarpellate species, Stelechantha ziamaeana, as examples. The presence of false septa (in addition to "true" septa) is characteristic for the entire tribe. They represent inward projections produced by the ovary wall which are only developed in the upper half of an ovary (thus seemingly doubling the number of locules present). The false septa are in contact with the "true" septum (or septa) only in the uppermost part of the ovary; lower down they gradually retreat and, from around mid-ovary, they are no longer discernible. The placentas, on which always many to very many ovules are borne, differ between bi- and more-carpellate taxa of the Pauridiantheae in their size, shape and orientation. It is noted that in the tetracarpellate Stelechantha ziamaeana the placentation is apparently quite similar to that described for the Urophylleae in that each locule has two placenta arms which are at least in part attached to the septa. In bicarpellate Pauridiantha, the placenta is a peltate, more or less heart-shaped structure.

10.2307/3668215|The Begoniaceae have a dubious position in most systems of classification. The family is usually placed together with the Datiscaceae in or near the Violales. The ontogeny of the staminate flower of some species of Begonia reveals great similarity with the floral development of members of the Papaveraceae. Flower buds arise in the axil of a bract between two bracteoles. The two sepals emerge successively and cover the apex as a saccate envelop. Next one pair of petals is initiated in Begoniaceae and two pairs in Papaveraceae. The androecium is always initiated as four stamens opposite the sepals or outer petals. The next stamen whorls arise as groups of two, four or six stamens, or in an irregular manner. In Papaver, a broad ringwall becomes covered by stamens starting with four stamen primordia alternating with the petals. The pentamerous perianth of pistillate flowers of Begonia shows a clear derivation from two trimerous whorls of tepals, as exemplified by transitional stages and floral ontogeny. The different types of androecia and their derived states are discussed, and a comparison of floral diagrams is made. The reputably primitive genus Hillebrandia (Begoniaceae) is considered to be an isolated offshoot, not much related to the rest of the family. In our opinion, the Begoniaceae are not well placed in Violales but should be transferred to the Magnoliidae near the Papaverales.
